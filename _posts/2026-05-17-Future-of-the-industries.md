I was recently at the NXT BLD conference in Westminster for construction tech startups. I've attended this conference annually for the last 4 years and this was its 10th anniversary year.

Up until now, it had always been an exciting event, buzzing with optimism for the future of our space. Construction had largely been left behind by the SaaS revolution, and these plucky upstart firms were organically developing a sense of forward momentum. This year, that optimistic atmosphere had changed noticeably. You've already seen the punchline coming; it's due to fears and insecurity stemming from the unstoppable rise of AI – what it means for us and our sector. If nothing else, it certainly *feels* like an existential threat.

All year, I've been thinking deeply about the kinds of impact AI might have on the workforce in general. What follow is my best guess as to where all of this is going. 

## Tech sector business models

### Business-to-business (B2B)

By size and revenue, the largest part of the tech sector exists as B2B enterprises. Often called SaaS firms, they sell licenses to their software and platforms which are often just part of the cost of doing business for their customers. I predict that these kinds of firms will be hit the hardest; some are already calling it the SaaSpocalypse.

At every commercial juncture, people on all sides can be seen sizing each other up, quietly wondering if they can automate everyone else's contributions. It's not surprising, really. The world of work is full of companies trying to find any commercial edge that they can. Software can constitute a large commercial outlay for an organisation; vibe coding potentially lets orgs reduce their cost, complexity and commercial reliance on tech firms.

This makes platforms vulnerable, they seem extremely uncompetitive compared to building your own technology now. 

* You're already bringing together competing companies on your platform; they share feature parity instead of having access to some special secret sauce that gives them a true competitive edge. Development of the platform is always a compromise between you and all of your competitors' needs. 
* All customers are tied to the platform's roadmap and development speed, which is always slower than it would be if developed in-house.
*  Annual price rises are locked in. You get another subscription to another walled garden that sees you as a resource to be mined (i.e. upsold).
* You are storing your valuable IP around products, materials, processes, patent information etc with an external company. Lots of trust required to do this as there is an element of risk involved.

Furthermore, each business only uses a small percentage, a few features of a platform that they need to conduct business. And each business uses a different set of features. The cost-benefit analysis is clear. Hire your own developers and do what you need in-house. It will be cheap, probably fairly fast, exactly the way you want it, with less with and a low ongoing maintenance cost. 

Converseley, SaaS companies need to spend a large part of their time, money and effort in developing their platforms. They are complex beasts, often trying to accommodate the needs of one or two specific verticals. Building a platform means building a big, wide beast of a thing. Industry standards around data security, financial regulations and internationalisation, performance, reliability, legal requirements / lesiglation. Very little of the effort expended is in doing what the firms expertise is in / the business domain. 

### Business-to-customer (B2C)

Firms operating under this model are less likely to be hit as hard. Your average customer or consumer is less technical. Most likely this business model is apps on a phone. There is a not insignificant amount of hassle with getting an app approved and made available on an app store, or even just loading it onto your own personal device. 

Furthermore, just because you can, it doesn't mean that everyone will develop their own apps. If you create the new Facebook or Spotify on your phone, nobody will care or use it. 

## Google Disco and where tech is heading

People often discredit "vibe coding" as being unfit for serious projects, it's shabby software that looks great but is poorly-structured or will soon descent into an unmaintainable chaos we'll come to regret. I think this is remarkably short-sighted and perhaps even denial as a coping mechanism. The term vibe-coding is only one year old. Automated software generation has moved forward leaps and bounds in the last 12 months. I believe that by 2027, AI will be capable of writing better software than most professionals could ever hope to output. The trend is heading rapidly towards instant, near-free software whenever we want it.  Google Disco is a great example of this. 

## Where value still remains

There is still value in tech. But no longer necessarily just in the code itself and the amount of complexity it takes to generate, test and maintain that code. 

There is value in rare IP. 

There is value in incumbency.

There is value in enormous complexity. Do not mistake complexity for lots of small simple tasks.

### Professional services firms

They are likely to be squeezed. Competition gets more intense. Customers expecting more, faster and for less. However, not fully automated. 

Humans still have a quality that AI cannot have, at least for now; they can be sued. People can act as a sponge for risk, taking ultimate responsibility. People can be insured, taken to court, fined or even presented with jail time. The legal sector is slow – lawsuits against tech/AI firms will remain niche and take a long time for case law to really establish itself in this fast-moving field. Nobody will insure an AI against its own misdeeds at least not yet.

## The hollowed-out company

How headless firms will just become services. Your entire company becomes bulletpoint in an agent's chain of reasoning. The great hollowing out. Almost all UI and company ceremonies and positions exist solely to facilitate other humans' needs. Where this is all headed. 

## The headless Internet

Headless Internet. What happens to ad revenue? If the Internet becomes an invisible layer, no humans eyeballs left. Can we influence agents with ads? That economy which paid for the entire web will also crumble.
